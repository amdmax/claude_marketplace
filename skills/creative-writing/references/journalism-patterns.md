# Journalism Patterns

## Overview

Caitlin Kelly's journalism applies her core techniques—vivid hooks, character-driven narratives, specific-to-universal flow—within journalistic constraints: verified facts, attributed sources, ethical reporting standards. This creates engaging storytelling that maintains credibility.

**Journalism adaptations:**
- Reported detail replaces imagined invention
- Sources must be attributed
- Objectivity maintained (show all sides, let readers interpret)
- Subjects are real people with verifiable experiences

## Profile Writing

### Pattern: Subject in Action

**Structure:**
1. **Opening:** Subject doing something revealing (specific moment, observable action)
2. **Background weave:** Who they are, how they got here (woven through action, not dumped)
3. **Environment reveals:** Where they work/live tells story through detail
4. **Subject's voice:** Direct quotes that show personality and perspective
5. **Broader context:** Why this person matters beyond themselves
6. **Closing:** Return to opening scene or new resonant moment

### Example: Profile of a Doctor

**Opening (subject in action):**
> At 11 PM on a Friday, Dr. Sarah Williams is still in her clinic office, returning patient calls she couldn't take during the day. She's been here since 7 AM, and her untouched lunch sits in the mini-fridge, the same turkey sandwich she brought Monday.

**Background (woven through action):**
> Williams, 42, has run this free clinic in downtown Detroit for eight years, since leaving a lucrative private practice. "I was making good money," she says, pulling up the next day's patient schedule on her computer. "But I wasn't making a difference." The schedule shows twenty-seven appointments, plus walk-ins.

**Environment reveals character:**
> Her office is small enough that she can touch both walls while sitting at her desk. Medical journals are stacked on the floor; patient thank-you cards cover one wall. The newest addition is a crayon drawing from a seven-year-old whose asthma she's been managing—the girl's first picture without an inhaler in her hand.

**Subject's voice (revealing quote):**
> "People ask if I regret the pay cut," Williams says, looking at that drawing. "Then I see patients like Maya, who was using her inhaler three times a day. Now she's playing soccer. You can't put a price on that. Well, you can. That's the problem."

**Broader context:**
> Williams is one of an estimated 1,400 physicians staffing free clinics across the United States, according to the National Association of Free and Charitable Clinics. They serve the 30 million Americans who earn too much to qualify for Medicaid but can't afford private insurance. In Michigan alone, free clinics see 180,000 patients annually.

**Closing (return to opening moment):**
> It's almost midnight when Williams returns the last call. She packs up that same turkey sandwich—tomorrow's lunch, she says—and locks the clinic door. Her phone buzzes immediately: a patient text about chest pain. She's typing back before she reaches her car.

### Profile Template

```markdown
## Opening Hook (100-150 words)
[Subject name] + [specific time/place] + [revealing action] + [observable detail that shows character/situation]

## Background Weave (200-300 words)
[Who they are - name, age, role]
[Key biographical detail that explains their current situation]
[Direct quote from subject explaining motivation/perspective]
[Specific detail showing their daily reality]

## Environment Section (150-200 words)
[Describe where they work/live with specific details]
[What's on the walls, desk, visible around them]
[Environmental details that reveal character choices/values]
[Sensory grounding - what's seen, heard, smelled]

## Subject Voice (200-300 words)
[Extended direct quotes showing personality]
[Attribute with action, not just "said"]
[Ask questions that get beneath surface answers]
[Include revealing anecdotes told by subject]

## Broader Context (150-200 words)
[Expert sources explaining why this individual story matters]
[Data/statistics showing pattern]
[Historical or systemic context]
[Other examples or comparative cases]

## Closing (100-150 words)
[Return to opening scene OR new scene that echoes theme]
[Final quote or observation that resonates]
[Avoid neat wrap-up; trust reader to extract meaning]
```

## Service Journalism

### Pattern: Problem → Human Face → Solution → Application

**Structure:**
1. **Open with person facing the problem** (specific individual, not abstract issue)
2. **Show the problem through their experience** (observable consequences)
3. **Explain the broader pattern** (data, expert sources)
4. **Present solutions/options** (actionable information)
5. **Return to opening person applying solution** (or acknowledging complexity)

### Example: Retirement Planning

**Person facing problem:**
> "I can't retire," says Tom Mitchell, sitting in the break room of the factory where he's worked for thirty-three years. "The math doesn't work. Even with Social Security, I'd be looking at $18,000 a year." He's sixty-two. Retirement age is three years away. He's already running the numbers backward, trying to figure out how long he needs to keep working.

**Problem through their experience:**
> Mitchell's company eliminated pensions five years ago for new hires, grandfathering in workers like him—but the pension pays only $600 monthly. His 401(k), hit hard in 2008, has $85,000. Social Security will pay $1,100 monthly if he waits until sixty-five. Total annual income: $21,000, in a city where average rent for a one-bedroom is $1,200 monthly.

**Broader pattern (with data):**
> Mitchell's situation is increasingly common. According to the National Institute on Retirement Security, forty-five percent of working-age Americans have no retirement savings at all. Among those who do, the median 401(k) balance for Americans aged 56-61 is $84,714—enough to provide roughly $300 monthly in retirement.

**Expert voice:**
> "People vastly underestimate how much they need to save," says Dr. Elena Rodriguez, an economist specializing in retirement planning at the University of Michigan. "The general rule is you'll need seventy to eighty percent of your pre-retirement income. Most people are saving enough for maybe thirty percent."

**Solutions/options:**
> Financial advisors recommend several strategies for people in Mitchell's situation:
>
> - **Delay Social Security:** Waiting until seventy increases monthly payments by thirty-two percent
> - **Part-time work:** Even two days weekly adds $10,000-15,000 annually
> - **Downsize housing:** Moving to a lower-cost area can cut expenses by forty percent
> - **Maximize catch-up contributions:** People over fifty can contribute an extra $7,500 annually to 401(k)s

**Application complexity:**
> For Mitchell, these solutions offer partial answers. Delaying Social Security means working longer—possible but exhausting at his age. Part-time work in his field is scarce. Downsizing would mean leaving the city where his daughter and grandkids live.
>
> "I'm not looking for sympathy," he says. "I'm just trying to figure out the math. And the math says I'm working until I'm seventy, minimum."

### Service Journalism Checklist

- [ ] **Human hook:** Opens with specific person facing the issue, not abstract problem statement?
- [ ] **Observable problem:** Shows impact through concrete details (numbers, consequences, daily reality)?
- [ ] **Verified data:** Includes statistics, research, expert sources to establish broader pattern?
- [ ] **Actionable solutions:** Provides specific steps/options readers can apply?
- [ ] **Honest complexity:** Acknowledges when solutions are partial, difficult, or context-dependent?
- [ ] **Return to human:** Closes with opening person considering/applying solutions?

## Feature Writing

### Pattern: Deep Dive with Multiple Voices

**Structure:**
1. **Opening scene:** Vivid moment that encapsulates the theme
2. **Primary subject:** First voice/story explored in depth
3. **Secondary voices:** Additional perspectives that complicate or deepen theme
4. **Expert context:** Research, data, historical background
5. **Synthesis:** How the pieces connect to reveal larger truth
6. **Closing:** Resonant moment that crystallizes theme

### Example: Gig Economy Workers (Excerpt)

**Opening scene:**
> At 6:15 AM, Marcus Johnson is already in his car, parked outside a Whole Foods, waiting for the app to ping him with a delivery order. He's been here for twenty minutes. Gas is running, heat is on—it's thirty-two degrees outside. He's calculating: if he gets an order in the next ten minutes, he'll break even on the gas he's burning. Fifteen minutes, he's losing money. At 6:27, the ping comes. $7.50 for a four-mile delivery. He accepts before reading the details. He always does.

**Primary subject (Marcus's full story):**
> Johnson, thirty-eight, drives for three delivery apps simultaneously—DoorDash, Instacart, and Uber Eats. He started two years ago after being laid off from a manufacturing job that paid $22 per hour with benefits. Now he works sixty hours weekly and makes roughly $15 per hour before expenses. Gas, car maintenance, and insurance—all out of pocket—drop that to around $11.
>
> "People think we're just driving around," he says, showing me his phone with all three apps open, each pinging chaotically. "But it's constant calculations. Which order pays best per mile? Which direction keeps me in a busy area? If I accept this $6 order, will I get stuck in a zone with no orders?"

**Secondary voice (different perspective):**
> Sarah Chen, forty-four, tells a different story. She drives for Lyft part-time, fifteen hours weekly, while working a full-time office job. "For me, it's extra income," she says. "I'm not trying to live on this. I'm saving for my daughter's college tuition." She makes roughly $18 per hour before expenses, driving only during surge pricing hours. She's strategic in ways Johnson can't afford to be—she can wait for good orders.

**Expert context:**
> The difference between Johnson and Chen illustrates what economists call the "gig economy split," according to Dr. Michael Torres at Stanford's Center for Work and Technology. "About forty percent of gig workers rely on it as primary income," Torres says. "They're the ones hurt most by low pay and lack of benefits. The other sixty percent treat it as supplemental. They have leverage to be selective. The platforms know this and price accordingly."

**Data grounding:**
> A 2023 study by the Economic Policy Institute found that after expenses, rideshare drivers earn a median of $11.77 per hour, while delivery drivers earn $9.91—both below minimum wage in many cities. More than half lack health insurance through gig work, and three-quarters have no retirement savings from these jobs.

### Feature Writing Checklist

- [ ] **Multiple voices:** Includes at least 2-3 individual subjects with different perspectives?
- [ ] **Depth over breadth:** Explores each subject's story in detail, not surface quotes?
- [ ] **Expert grounding:** Includes research, data, or expert analysis for context?
- [ ] **Thematic coherence:** All voices/sections connect to central theme?
- [ ] **Scene-based:** Uses vivid scenes rather than summary throughout?
- [ ] **Balanced perspective:** Shows complexity, not single narrative?

## Business Reporting

### Pattern: Human Impact of Economic/Corporate News

Kelly's approach to business reporting humanizes economic and corporate stories by showing how business decisions affect real people.

**Structure:**
1. **Worker/customer perspective:** Open with individual experiencing the business reality
2. **Company context:** Explain the business decision/situation
3. **Economic data:** Provide financial/market context
4. **Stakeholder voices:** Management, workers, analysts, customers
5. **Impact analysis:** Who benefits, who's hurt, what are trade-offs
6. **Closing:** Return to human perspective with forward-looking observation

### Example: Company Layoffs (Excerpt)

**Worker perspective:**
> Maria Santos gets the email at 6:47 AM: "Your position has been eliminated." She's worked in customer support at TechCorp for six years, promoted twice, never missed a performance target. The email includes a Zoom link for a 9 AM meeting with HR. She has two hours to understand that her job is gone.

**Company context:**
> TechCorp announced 2,000 layoffs yesterday, eleven percent of its workforce, part of what CEO David Chen called "strategic restructuring to improve operational efficiency." The company posted $800 million in profits last quarter, up eighteen percent year-over-year. Its stock rose four percent on news of the layoffs.

**Economic data:**
> The tech industry has cut 140,000 jobs in the past year, according to Layoffs.fyi, even as many companies report record profits. The pattern reflects a shift from pandemic hiring surges to what analysts call "efficiency optimization"—cutting costs to boost profit margins in a higher interest rate environment.

**Management voice:**
> In an interview, Chen defends the decision: "We over-hired during the pandemic. These cuts are painful but necessary to ensure long-term sustainability and shareholder value. We're focusing on AI-driven solutions that can handle many customer service functions more efficiently."

**Worker impact:**
> For Santos, "operational efficiency" means her severance package is six weeks of pay—her manager told her it would have been twelve weeks if she'd made it to seven years. Her health insurance ends in thirty days. She's the primary earner in her family; her husband freelances irregularly.

**Closing:**
> Santos is updating her resume, the first time in six years. The "Skills" section is difficult to write. How do you describe being good at a job that a company decided you don't need to do anymore?

### Business Reporting Checklist

- [ ] **Human-centered opening:** Starts with individual experiencing business decision?
- [ ] **Balanced stakeholders:** Includes management/executive perspectives alongside workers?
- [ ] **Financial grounding:** Provides specific financial data, not vague "struggling" or "thriving"?
- [ ] **Impact analysis:** Shows who benefits and who's hurt by business decisions?
- [ ] **Systemic context:** Connects individual company to broader economic patterns?
- [ ] **Avoids jargon:** Explains business terminology through concrete examples?

## General Journalism Techniques

### Interviewing for Kelly's Style

**Ask questions that elicit stories, not opinions:**

❌ Weak: "How do you feel about working long hours?"
✓ Strong: "Walk me through a typical day. When do you arrive, what do you do first, when do you leave?"

❌ Weak: "Is work-life balance hard for you?"
✓ Strong: "Tell me about the last family event you missed because of work."

❌ Weak: "What challenges do you face?"
✓ Strong: "Tell me about a specific time something went wrong. What happened?"

**Follow-up questions matter:**
- "Can you give me a specific example?"
- "What did that look like?"
- "How much did that cost?"
- "How did you feel in that moment?"

### Observation During Reporting

**Note specific, sensory details:**
- What's on their desk/walls/surroundings
- How they move, speak, interact with space
- Physical appearance (without judgment)
- Environmental sounds, smells, visual elements
- Numbers visible (time stamps, quantities, measurements)

**Record quotes precisely:**
- Use recording device with permission
- Note exact phrasing, including fragments and colloquialisms
- Capture interruptions, pauses, emotional shifts
- Distinguish between on-record and off-record

### Ethical Considerations

**Transparency:**
- Identify yourself as a journalist
- Explain how information will be used
- Get informed consent for quotes and stories
- Clarify what's on-record, off-record, background

**Accuracy:**
- Verify all facts independently
- Confirm spellings, titles, dates, numbers
- Read back quotes to sources when needed
- Correct errors promptly

**Fairness:**
- Represent subjects accurately, even if unflattering
- Include context that complicates simple narratives
- Give subjects chance to respond to criticism
- Show complexity, not caricature

## Attribution Patterns

### Direct Quote (for voice, opinion, perspective)
> "I can't retire," says Tom Mitchell. "The math doesn't work."

### Paraphrase with Attribution (for facts from source)
> According to Mitchell, his pension will pay only $600 monthly, and his 401(k) balance is $85,000.

### Expert Sourcing (for context, analysis)
> Dr. Elena Rodriguez, an economist specializing in retirement planning at the University of Michigan, says people "vastly underestimate" their retirement needs.

### Data Attribution (for statistics)
> According to the National Institute on Retirement Security, forty-five percent of working-age Americans have no retirement savings.

### Observation (for reported detail, no attribution needed)
> Mitchell's untouched lunch sits in the break room fridge, the same sandwich he brought Monday.

## Journalism Workflow Summary

1. **Pre-reporting:** Research subject/topic, identify potential human subjects, prepare questions
2. **Reporting:** Interview subjects (prioritize stories over opinions), observe environment, gather documents/data
3. **Verification:** Confirm facts, check spellings/titles, verify data sources
4. **Structuring:** Identify strongest hook, map specific-to-universal flow, plan voice integration
5. **Drafting:** Write with Kelly's techniques (vivid hook, conversational voice, grounded detail)
6. **Fact-checking:** Re-verify all facts, re-confirm quotes, check attributions
7. **Editing:** Strengthen voice, vary rhythm, ensure ethical balance
