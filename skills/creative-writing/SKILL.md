## Overview

This skill generates creative content across multiple genres using **Caitlin Kelly's journalistic writing techniques** as the foundation. Kelly, a veteran journalist and author, has developed a signature approach that makes readers care through:

- **Vivid scene-setting hooks** that drop readers into immediate, specific experiences
- **Character-driven narratives** that humanize subjects through observable details and actions
- **Specific-to-universal flow** that moves from concrete anecdotes to broader themes
- **Conversational voice** that balances accessibility with credibility
- **Grounded detail** rooted in sensory observation and reported facts

While Kelly's work is primarily journalistic (profiles, features, essays), her techniques adapt powerfully to fiction, educational content, and general creative writing. This skill applies her methods across all these genres, maintaining her core principles while adjusting for genre-specific needs.

## Caitlin Kelly's Core Techniques

### 1. Vivid Scene-Setting Hooks
Open with specific, immediate detail that places readers in a moment. Not abstract generalizations—concrete sensory experiences.

**Example:** "At 6 AM on a Tuesday, Maria Chen is already on her third espresso, debugging code that crashed the night before while her daughter's forgotten lunch sits on the counter."

### 2. Character-Driven Approach
Humanize subjects through actions, environment, and observable details. Show people in motion, not in stasis.

### 3. Specific-to-Universal Flow
Start with concrete anecdotes, explore their particulars, extract broader implications, connect to universal themes, then ground with more examples.

### 4. Conversational Voice
Accessible yet authoritative. Use active voice, varied sentence rhythms, concrete nouns over abstractions. Write as if explaining to an intelligent friend.

### 5. Grounded Detail
Root every assertion in sensory observation, reported facts, or tangible specifics. Avoid floating abstractions.

## Generation Workflow

Ask clarifying questions if not provided:

- **Topic/subject:** What are you writing about?
- **Genre:** Journalism (profile, feature, essay), fiction (short story, novel chapter), educational (explainer, tutorial), or general creative writing?
- **Audience:** Who is this for? What's their background/expertise?
- **Length:** Approximate word count or scope?
- **Specific goals:** Any particular constraints, themes, or objectives?

### Step 3: Structure the Narrative

Apply Kelly's specific-to-universal flow pattern:

1. **Identify the concrete hook:** What person, scene, or moment will open the piece? Choose something immediate, specific, vivid.

2. **Map the anecdotal journey:** What observable details, actions, or reported facts will explore this concrete starting point?

3. **Identify the broader theme:** What universal principle, broader implication, or larger truth does this specific story illustrate?

4. **Plan the bridges:** How will you transition from specific to universal and back? Kelly uses natural progressions—concrete observation leads to reflection, which connects to principle, then returns to grounded example.

5. **Choose structural milestones:** For longer pieces, map multiple specific-to-universal cycles rather than a single arc.

### Step 4: Generate Content

Draft the content using Kelly's techniques:

**Opening (First 100-200 words):**
- Start with a vivid, specific hook—a person in action, a revealing moment, a concrete scene
- Use sensory details: what's visible, audible, tangible
- Avoid abstractions, throat-clearing, or context dumps
- Drop the reader into immediate experience

**Body (Middle sections):**
- Maintain conversational voice—active verbs, varied sentence rhythms, concrete nouns
- Ground every paragraph in observable detail or reported fact
- Build specific → universal flow: concrete anecdote → explore particulars → extract implications → connect to theme → return to concrete examples
- For journalism: weave background information naturally through character actions and environment
- For fiction: reveal character interiority through actions and reactions, not exposition
- For educational: use real-world scenarios to illustrate learning principles before abstract explanation

**Dialogue (if applicable):**
- Natural speech rhythms, not formal prose
- Purposeful—reveals character, advances narrative, or illuminates theme
- Attributed with action, not just "said"

**Closing:**
- Return to concrete detail or image
- Echo opening hook or introduce resonant final observation
- Avoid neat summarization—trust the reader to extract meaning

### Step 5: Review & Refine

Use this self-review checklist to verify Kelly's style:

- [ ] **Hook quality:** Does the opening drop readers into immediate, specific experience within the first 2-3 sentences?
- [ ] **Voice consistency:** Is the tone conversational yet credible? Active voice predominant? Concrete over abstract?
- [ ] **Grounded detail:** Is every assertion rooted in sensory observation, reported fact, or tangible specific? No floating abstractions?
- [ ] **Specific-to-universal flow:** Does the piece move from concrete anecdotes to broader implications and back? Not abstract throughout?
- [ ] **Character humanity:** Do subjects/characters feel human—shown through actions, environment, observable details rather than labels?
- [ ] **Sentence rhythm:** Varied sentence lengths? Mix of short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones?
- [ ] **Genre appropriateness:** Does the adaptation fit the genre (journalistic rigor for journalism, narrative interiority for fiction, accessible explanation for educational)?

**Revision focus:**
- Strengthen weak hooks—add specificity, sensory detail, immediacy
- Cut abstractions—replace with concrete examples, actions, observations
- Smooth transitions—ensure natural bridges from specific to universal
- Sharpen voice—eliminate passive constructions, vague nouns, formal distance

## Examples

### Example 1: Educational Content (Before/After)

**Before (Generic, abstract):**
> API rate limiting is an important concept in web development. It prevents servers from being overwhelmed by too many requests. Developers should implement rate limiting to protect their applications from abuse and ensure fair resource allocation.

**After (Kelly's style—specific, human-centered):**
> At 2:47 AM, Sarah's phone buzzes with an alert: her startup's API just received 10,000 requests in three seconds. She watches the server logs scroll past—same endpoint, same pattern, over and over. By the time she kills the runaway script, her monthly AWS bill has jumped $800.

> This is what happens without rate limiting, the bouncer at the API door who decides who gets in and how often. Sarah's experience isn't unique—every developer learns this lesson eventually, usually at 2 AM.

**Techniques applied:**
- Specific scene-setting hook (Sarah at 2:47 AM)
- Concrete problem illustration (actual costs, observable details)
- Metaphor grounded in familiar concept (bouncer)
- Specific-to-universal transition (Sarah's experience → broader lesson)

### Example 2: Fiction Opening (Before/After)

**Before (Distant, expository):**
> Marcus was a lonely man who lived in a small apartment in the city. He had always been shy and found it difficult to connect with others. One day, he discovered something that would change his life.

**After (Kelly's style—immediate, character-driven):**
> Marcus finds the letter wedged behind the radiator while fishing out a dropped sock—yellowed envelope, handwritten address, postmarked 1987. His apartment building is old enough for surprises like this: mouse droppings in the cupboards, water stains mapping ancient leaks, and now this, someone's forgotten correspondence.

> He shouldn't read it. He knows this. But the envelope is already open, torn at one corner, and the first line is visible: "I'm writing this because I can't say it to your face."

**Techniques applied:**
- Immediate scene (Marcus in action, not summarized backstory)
- Environmental detail reveals character (observant, notices building's history)
- Sensory grounding (visual details of letter)
- Hook creates narrative tension (mysterious letter, ethical dilemma)

### Example 3: Profile Opening (Journalism)

**Before (Resume-style):**
> Dr. Jennifer Wu is a renowned marine biologist specializing in coral reef restoration. She has published over 40 papers and led multiple research expeditions. Her work focuses on developing techniques to help damaged reefs recover.

**After (Kelly's style—person in action):**
> Dr. Jennifer Wu is underwater at dawn, photographing a patch of coral the size of a dinner plate. She's been tracking this section of reef for three years, documenting its slow recovery after a bleaching event. The water is 78 degrees—warm enough to worry about, but not catastrophic. Not yet.

> Back on the research boat, she downloads the images and pulls up comparison shots from last month. The coral's color has deepened slightly, a good sign. She marks it in her field notes with cautious optimism, the kind scientists develop after watching too many promising experiments fail.

**Techniques applied:**
- Person in action hook (Wu underwater, working)
- Environmental detail (water temperature, visual observation)
- Weaves expertise into action (tracking, comparison, interpretation)
- Shows character through behavior (cautious optimism, scientific temperament)

## Edge Case Handling

### Very Short Content (<200 words)
For short-form content, compress the workflow:
- **Focus:** Strong hook + one concrete detail + quick universal connection
- **Skip:** Elaborate anecdotal journey, multiple specific-to-universal cycles
- **Example:** Social media posts, microcontent, brief explanations

### Style-Incompatible Requests
Some requests don't suit Kelly's narrative approach (e.g., API documentation, technical specifications, formal reports):
- **Suggest alternatives:** Narrative tutorial instead of technical docs, case study instead of specification
- **Offer hybrid:** Kelly-style introduction + traditional format body
- **Clarify:** Explain why narrative approach may not fit formal/technical requirements

### Unclear Genre
If the user's genre isn't clear:
- **Present options:** Describe how each genre would approach the topic
- **Ask clarifying questions:** Goal, audience, context, constraints
- **Offer recommendation:** Based on topic and apparent intent

### Complex Multi-Genre Requests
For requests that blend genres (e.g., "educational fiction" or "journalistic essay"):
- **Identify primary genre:** Which genre's goals dominate?
- **Apply hybrid approach:** Use primary genre's pattern with secondary genre's techniques
- **Reference `general-patterns.md`:** For flexible blended approaches
